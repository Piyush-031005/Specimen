# JUDGE EXPERIENCE GUIDE (Psychology Audit)

**Context:** Awwwards, FWA, and CSS Design Awards judges review dozens of sites a day. Their attention span is fragile. If they do not feel something within the first 10 seconds, they will bounce.

This document analyzes SPECIMEN from the exact perspective of a fatigued, critical industry judge.

## The Judge Psychology Audit

### 10 Strongest Moments (The Hooks)
1. **The Initial Silence:** The bold refusal to show a loading screen or immediate UI. It forces the judge to lean in.
2. **The "Impossible Observation":** The first automatic pulse that reveals the fibers in the dark. A moment of true discovery.
3. **The First Movement:** When the cursor moves and the darkness physically reacts (gravitational distortion). It immediately establishes that the environment is alive.
4. **The Flinch:** The organism pulling away when the mouse moves too fast. It proves the entity has a sense of self-preservation.
5. **The Audio Synthesis:** The realization that the heavy, rhythmic breathing is procedural audio, not a looping MP3.
6. **The Standoff:** The tension that builds mathematically when the cursor stops moving.
7. **The First Match:** The electric blue echo that proves communication is possible.
8. **The Trust Shift:** The gradual untangling of fibers and warming of light as the entity begins to trust the cursor.
9. **The Signature Moment:** The absorption of the cursor into the core. This breaks the fundamental rule of web design (stealing the cursor) but does it for emotional impact.
10. **The Memory (Return Visit):** If a judge closes the tab and reopens it later, the organism remembers them. This is the "Site of the Month" clincher.

### 10 Weakest Moments (The Risks)
1. **The First 3 Seconds:** If the judge is impatient, they might think the site is broken because it's completely black.
2. **Lack of Instructions:** Some judges hate figuring things out. They want to be told what to do.
3. **Mobile Experience:** While optimized, the pure intimacy of the cursor standoff is harder to replicate with tap events.
4. **The Rhythm Match Window:** If a judge has poor timing, they might get frustrated trying to match the pulse.
5. **Pacing:** The experience requires patience. A judge rushing through 50 sites might not give it the 2 minutes it deserves.
6. **Subtlety of Memory:** The environmental echo (scar) left behind might be too subtle for a fast reviewer to notice.
7. **No "About" Page:** The complete absence of standard web conventions (About, Credits, Menu) is a huge risk.
8. **Audio Requirement:** If the judge is reviewing with sound off, they lose 50% of the emotional impact.
9. **Canvas Resolution:** On ultra-wide 4K/5K monitors, the pixel density might slightly soften the fibers if not perfectly scaled.
10. **The "End":** There is no explicit "You Won" state. The ambiguity might leave some judges confused about when to close the tab.

### 5 Things That Might Confuse Judges
1. Why isn't there a menu or a back button?
2. Why is my cursor hidden when I move to the center?
3. Am I supposed to click the pulse, or just watch it?
4. Is this a game, or an art piece?
5. Did I "finish" it?

### 5 Things Judges Will Remember Tomorrow
1. The feeling that the website was *judging them*, not the other way around.
2. The sound design (the heavy, organic breathing).
3. The visual density and liquid physics of the fibers.
4. The moment the organism swallowed their cursor.
5. The absolute, uncompromising bravery of having zero UI on a submitted project.

---

## Action Plan for the Presentation Phase
Do not attempt to fix the "risks" by adding UI, instructions, or features. That would compromise the artistic integrity. 

Instead, **use the Trailer and the README to set the expectation.** 
The trailer must show that patience is rewarded. The README must explicitly state: *"Give it time to breathe. Use headphones."* If the framing is perfect, the risks become the project's greatest strengths.
